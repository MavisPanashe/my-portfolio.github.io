Reflective Statement

Participating in this discussion moved me from a rule‑based view of research ethics to a more nuanced appreciation of ethical practice as an ongoing process. At first I focused almost exclusively on compliance: cite the ACM and RSS codes, disclose all results, avoid cherry‑picking. Feedback from student peers broadened that lens in three key ways.

1  Framing versus falsification

A student peer’s concept of “epistemic manipulation” taught me that ethical breaches can occur without touching the data. I had underestimated how narrative framing alone can mislead stakeholders. This insight now informs how I write summaries, slide decks and even email updates; I look for hidden emphases that could distort the message.

2  Reasonable foresight and shared accountability

Another student peer referenced the UKRIO Code of Practice and reframed safeguards—preregistration, open repositories and disclosure clauses—as duties rather than extras. I now see accountability as distributed across researcher, funder and regulator, with each node responsible for anticipating misuse. This has already influenced a current group project where we negotiated explicit publication terms before analysing client data.

3  Practical leverage

A different student peer challenged the pragmatics of “forcing” a manufacturer to publish unfavourable results. In response I explored contract‑drafting strategies and realised that many ethical problems can be mitigated at the project‑design stage, long before any moral dilemma arises.

Beyond content knowledge, I strengthened my Harvard‑style referencing skills by iterating on feedback and ensuring every in‑text citation matched the reference list. Most importantly, I learned that collaborative critique within a community of student peers is not adversarial; it is an engine for deeper insight and professional growth.
