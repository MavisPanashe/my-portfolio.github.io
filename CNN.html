Biased and wrong? Facial recognition tech in the dock

When I contemplate facial recognition technology, which employs Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to identify or verify individuals based on unique facial attributes, I am both fascinated and apprehensive. This technology, defined as the automated process of detecting and distinguishing one face from another, possesses significant potential to streamline tasks ranging from unlocking personal devices to bolstering security protocols in public arenas. However, I harbour concerns regarding the privacy and autonomy of individuals subjected to these systems. Privacy, construed as the state of being free from public attention or unsanctioned observation, is jeopardised when cameras capture facial images without explicit consent. I find it unsettling to imagine my face being analysed by algorithms in transportation hubs or retail locations, with little transparency about data storage or the purpose of such analysis. In my view, this sector of omnipresent monitoring could engender an environment of perpetual vigilance, undermining the spontaneity of public life. Moreover, I worry about bias, often defined as a prejudice in favour of or against one person or group, especially in a way considered unfair. CNNs can inadvertently perpetuate discrimination if their training data fails to represent the full spectrum of human diversity. In practice, misidentification or skewed results could have grave repercussions, particularly in areas like law enforcement or employment, where critical decisions hinge on model outputs (Wall, 2019). Another dilemma pertains to accountability, meaning the obligation or willingness to accept responsibility or to account for one’s actions. CNNs, frequently described as “black boxes” (Buhrmester, Münch and Arens, 2021), do not readily divulge how they arrive at specific classifications, creating a barrier to contesting or rectifying erroneous or unjust decisions. This opacity can breed mistrust and erode confidence in systems that increasingly permeate our daily interactions. To reconcile these tensions, I believe it is imperative to establish stringent ethical guidelines, transparent protocols, and robust regulatory frameworks. Such measures would safeguard the rights and freedoms of individuals, ensuring that facial recognition technology, while undeniably impressive, does not jeopardise the values of fairness, privacy, and autonomy that I hold dear.
