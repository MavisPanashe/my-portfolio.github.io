Literature Review

Attachments

  • Research Proposal Review (DOCX)

Starting this project with an outline and literature‑review plan, written before the full proposal, proved unexpectedly valuable. It made me pause and clarify both what I wanted to learn and how I might discover it, instead of jumping straight into writing.

What went well

  • Early scoping of the topic. Drafting a concise focus statement (“digital transformation in the NHS through core information systems”) kept the scope under control. Every later element—objectives, search terms, inclusion rules—linked back to that single sentence.
  • Selecting a guiding framework. Committing to CFIR or NASSS from the outset provided instant structure; interview guides, extraction tables and slide headings now have a clear anchor.
  • Creating a realistic timeline. Sketching a nine‑week timetable highlighted pressure points such as screening and extraction, prompting me to reserve study blocks in advance. The project already feels more manageable than if the schedule were vague.

Challenges encountered

  • Balancing depth with simplicity. My first outline was overly wordy; trimming it showed what information I truly needed.
  • Anticipating evidence gaps felt speculative—for example, guessing that economic studies might be scarce—yet I reminded myself the outline is only a plan.
  • Translating mixed‑methods jargon into plain language took effort but will help when explaining the project to non‑academic stakeholders.

Key learning points

  • Front‑loaded thinking cuts later rewriting; a strong outline means fewer structural edits down the line.
  • Frameworks actively guide search strings, interview questions and data‑extraction fields rather than sitting on the shelf as decoration.
  • Reflection improves with breaks; after leaving the outline for a day, I returned and spotted redundant aims and tightened wording.

Skills sharpened or identified for development

  • Concise writing that distills technical ideas into brief statements.
  • Project‑management planning, including drafting a basic Gantt chart.
  • Data‑governance awareness, flagging ethics and Caldicott‑guardian tasks early so they will not delay progress.

Next steps informed by this reflection

  • Lock the framework choice—most likely CFIR—so every other component aligns.
  • Draft a one‑page review protocol to register methods and avoid mid‑study drift.
  • Prepare data‑extraction templates while concepts remain fresh.
  • Schedule short reflection checkpoints at key milestones (after screening, after extraction) to catch issues quickly.

Producing the outline and preliminary review felt like laying foundations; once the final structure stands these building blocks may be invisible, yet they are essential for stability and direction.
